title: "FAQ: How often must the WVPP be reviewed?"
faqCategory: sb-553
excerpt: "The Workplace Violence Prevention Plan must be reviewed annually at minimum, plus after any incident, identified deficiency, or new hazard. Document every review."
---

Your Workplace Violence Prevention Plan is not a document you write once and file away. It is a living document with mandatory review triggers.

**Minimum review frequency: annually.**

At least once every twelve months, you must review the entire WVPP for effectiveness, accuracy, and completeness. This is the baseline -- the absolute minimum.

**Additional mandatory reviews after:**

- **A workplace violence incident.** Any incident -- whether it resulted in injury or not -- triggers a plan review. Did the plan work? Did employees follow the procedures? Were there gaps? Fix them.

- **An identified deficiency.** If an internal review, an employee report, or any other mechanism reveals a weakness in the plan, you review and update immediately. Not at the next annual review. Now.

- **New workplace violence hazards.** Changed operations, new work locations, staffing changes, new types of public interaction -- anything that introduces a new hazard profile requires a plan review.

- **Changes in work practices or procedures.** If the way work gets done changes, the plan must reflect those changes.

**What a proper review looks like:**

A review is not reading the document and saying "looks fine." A proper review involves:

- Evaluating whether the plan addresses all current hazards
- Checking that emergency procedures still match current building layout, staffing, and contacts
- Verifying that reporting procedures are still functional and accessible
- Confirming that training reflects the current version of the plan
- Updating the hazard assessment if conditions have changed

**Document the review itself.**

Record the date of each review, who conducted it, what was evaluated, and what changes were made. This documentation is your proof of compliance. When Cal/OSHA asks "when was this plan last reviewed?" you need a specific date and a record to back it up.

A stale plan is almost as bad as no plan at all.
