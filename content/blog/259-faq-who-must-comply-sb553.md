title: "FAQ: Who must comply with SB 553?"
faqCategory: sb-553
excerpt: "Nearly every California employer with on-site employees must comply with SB 553. The exceptions are narrow and specific."
---

Here is the straight answer: if you have employees who physically show up to a workplace in California, you almost certainly must comply with SB 553.

The law requires every California employer to establish, implement, and maintain a Workplace Violence Prevention Plan. This is not a suggestion. It is not a "best practice." It is a legal mandate with teeth.

**Who is covered:**

- Private employers with employees working on-site in California
- State and local government employers
- Any business with at least one non-remote employee physically present at a worksite

**Who is exempt (and it is a short list):**

- Employees who telework from a location of their choice that is not controlled by the employer
- Healthcare facilities already covered under Cal/OSHA Section 3342 (they have their own, stricter workplace violence standard)
- Law enforcement agencies
- Corrections facilities
- Employers with employees covered by a collective bargaining agreement that provides equivalent protections

Notice what is NOT on that exemption list: small businesses. There is no employee count threshold. Five employees or five hundred -- the requirement is the same. A two-person shop with a storefront must comply just like a company with regional offices across the state.

The critical mistake I see business owners make is assuming this law targets "big companies" or "dangerous industries." Wrong. SB 553 is industry-agnostic. Retail, professional services, restaurants, construction, tech companies with offices -- all covered.

If you have a physical workplace in California and people show up to work there, you need a Workplace Violence Prevention Plan. Period.
