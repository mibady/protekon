# Warehouse and Distribution Compliance Playbook

Warehousing looks simple from the outside. Goods come in. Goods go out. Forklifts move things. People pick things. How complicated can the compliance picture be?

Complicated enough to generate one of the fastest-growing citation rates in California. Cal/OSHA has identified warehousing and distribution as an enforcement priority, driven by the explosive growth of e-commerce fulfillment, the injury rates that accompany high-volume operations, and the public attention that warehouse working conditions have received in recent years.

If you operate a warehouse in California — whether it is a 10,000-square-foot parts distribution operation or a 500,000-square-foot fulfillment center — your compliance obligations are broader and more aggressively enforced than they were five years ago. This playbook covers the full stack: the eight baseline programs applied to warehouse operations, the warehouse-specific programs that generate the most citations, and the operational practices that keep your facility on the right side of an inspection.

## The 8 Baseline Programs (Warehouse Context)

**1. IIPP.** Warehouse IIPPs must address the specific hazard profile of materials handling operations: powered industrial truck hazards, falling objects, struck-by hazards, ergonomic injuries from repetitive lifting, slip/trip/fall hazards from packaging materials and wet floors, and dock operations. Your IIPP must also address the shift structure of your operation — if you run 24/7, your safety program must be operational 24/7, not just during the shift when the safety manager works.

**2. WVPP.** Warehouse violence risk factors include high-pressure productivity environments, temporary staffing agencies placing unfamiliar workers alongside permanent staff, isolated areas (deep racking aisles, mezzanine levels, exterior dock areas during night shifts), and employee frustration related to pace-of-work expectations. Your WVPP must address how employees in isolated areas summon help.

**3. Heat Illness Prevention.** Indoor heat illness is the sleeper issue in warehousing. California's indoor heat standard applies when indoor temperatures reach 82 degrees. Most warehouses — especially those without climate control, those with dock doors open to summer heat, and those with equipment generating waste heat — exceed this threshold for months each year. If you do not have an indoor heat illness program for your warehouse, you do not have a complete compliance program.

**4. HazCom.** Warehouses handle chemicals as stored products and as operational supplies (cleaning agents, forklift battery acid, lubricants, dock sealants). Your HazCom program must cover both — the chemicals your employees use in operations and the chemicals that pass through your facility as inventory, if employees could be exposed during a spill, leak, or damaged container event.

**5. Emergency Action Plan.** Warehouse emergencies include fires (especially in high-rack storage), structural collapse of racking systems, hazardous material spills from damaged inventory, forklift collisions, and natural disasters affecting large-span roof structures. Your evacuation plan must account for the fact that warehouse layouts change as inventory shifts — exit paths that are clear today may be blocked by inventory tomorrow.

**6. Incident Investigation.** Warehouse incident investigations commonly reveal pattern causes: inadequate training, production pressure overriding safety procedures, equipment maintenance deferrals, and inadequate pedestrian/vehicle separation. Your investigation process must address whether the incident reflects a systemic problem or an isolated event — and the answer is almost always systemic.

**7. OSHA 300 Log.** Warehouse operations have high musculoskeletal injury rates. Ensure ergonomic injuries are properly classified. Repetitive strain injuries, back injuries from lifting, and cumulative trauma disorders are recordable if they meet the recording criteria — do not under-record because the injury developed over time rather than from a single event.

**8. Training Records.** Warehouse training requirements include forklift certification (initial and triennial renewal), HazCom, ergonomic lifting techniques, dock safety, pedestrian safety, and emergency procedures. For operations using temporary workers, the host employer is responsible for site-specific training — do not assume the staffing agency trained the worker on your facility's hazards.

## Warehouse-Specific Programs

### Forklift / Powered Industrial Truck (PIT) Certification

Section 3650-3668 (California), 29 CFR 1910.178 (federal). Forklift violations are the number one citation category in warehousing. Not because the standard is obscure — because compliance requires ongoing management that most warehouses fail to maintain.

**Initial certification.** Every forklift operator must be evaluated and certified before operating a powered industrial truck. Certification includes formal instruction (classroom or equivalent), practical training, and an evaluation of the operator's performance in the workplace. All three components are required — watching a video and signing a form is not certification.

**Evaluation content.** The operator must demonstrate competence in: pre-operation inspection, safe startup and shutdown, traveling with and without loads, turning, maneuvering in tight spaces, load handling (picking, placing, stacking), operating on grades, operating near pedestrians, refueling or recharging, and any operation specific to your facility.

**Truck-specific certification.** An operator certified on a sit-down counterbalanced forklift is not certified on a reach truck, an order picker, or a pallet jack. Certification is truck-type specific. If your facility uses multiple truck types, each operator must be certified on each type they operate.

**Triennial re-evaluation.** Every three years, every operator must be re-evaluated. This is not a formality — it is a hands-on performance evaluation. Operators who have developed unsafe habits over three years need correction, not rubber-stamping.

**Trigger-based re-evaluation.** Between triennial evaluations, operators must be re-evaluated if they are observed operating unsafely, involved in an incident or near-miss, assigned to a different truck type, or working in conditions that have changed significantly (new racking layout, new dock configuration, etc.).

**Daily pre-operation inspections.** Every forklift must be inspected before each shift. Brakes, steering, controls, tires, forks, mast, hydraulics, lights, horn, backup alarm, seatbelt. Document the inspection. A forklift that was not inspected before use is a forklift that should not be in operation — and an operator who skipped the inspection is an operator who needs retraining.

### Dock Safety

Loading docks are among the most hazardous areas in a warehouse. The combination of powered industrial trucks, tractor-trailers, pedestrians, elevation changes, weather exposure, and time pressure creates a concentration of risk that demands specific safety protocols.

**Dock lock / trailer restraint.** Before any forklift enters a trailer for loading or unloading, the trailer must be secured against movement. Wheel chocks alone are insufficient — a dock lock, trailer restraint, or vehicle restraint system that physically prevents the trailer from moving is the standard. The number of warehouse fatalities caused by trailers pulling away from the dock while a forklift is inside the trailer is staggering and entirely preventable.

**Dock leveler safety.** Dock levelers must be rated for the loads they will carry (forklift weight plus load weight), maintained in proper working condition, and inspected regularly. A malfunctioning dock leveler can drop a loaded forklift four feet onto concrete — the resulting injuries are severe to fatal.

**Dock edge protection.** Open dock doors without trailers present create fall hazards. Guardrails, chains, or other barriers must prevent employees and equipment from falling off the dock edge. This includes dock doors that are opened for ventilation.

**Lighting.** Trailer interiors are dark. Dock areas transition from outdoor light to warehouse light. Both conditions require supplemental lighting to prevent struck-by and trip/fall hazards.

### Pedestrian Traffic Management

The separation of pedestrians and powered industrial trucks is the single most important operational safety practice in a warehouse. Pedestrian strikes by forklifts produce fatalities, amputations, and crushing injuries with alarming frequency.

**Designated pedestrian pathways.** Marked floor lanes, separated from forklift travel lanes by physical barriers or clearly marked boundaries. Pedestrian paths must connect all employee destinations — break rooms, restrooms, offices, parking lots — without requiring employees to cross forklift lanes unsafely.

**Intersections.** Where pedestrian and forklift paths must cross, install mirrors, warning signs, stop signs, or traffic signals. Blind corners at rack aisle ends are the highest-risk intersections in any warehouse.

**High-visibility requirements.** Pedestrians in warehouse areas must wear high-visibility vests. This is not optional in areas where forklifts operate — a pedestrian in dark clothing between racking aisles is invisible to a forklift operator until it is too late.

**Visitor and contractor protocols.** Every non-employee who enters the warehouse floor must receive a safety orientation: pedestrian paths, forklift awareness, PPE requirements, emergency exits. A visitor who wanders into a forklift lane is your liability, not theirs.

### Pallet Rack Inspections

Pallet racking is structural infrastructure. It holds thousands of pounds of inventory at heights of 20 feet or more. When racking fails, it fails catastrophically — a progressive collapse can cascade through an entire rack row, dropping tons of inventory on anyone below.

**Inspection frequency.** Weekly visual inspections by trained personnel. Annual comprehensive inspections by a qualified rack engineer or inspection service.

**What to inspect:**
- Upright frames: plumb, no visible damage, base plates anchored
- Beams: level, properly seated in connectors, safety clips installed, no deflection under load
- Beam connectors: not deformed, fully engaged, safety pins in place
- Column damage: dents, bends, tears, scrapes from forklift impact — any column damage that exceeds the manufacturer's damage criteria must be immediately unloaded and the column replaced or repaired
- Load capacity signage: posted on every rack section, not exceeded

**The forklift impact problem.** Most rack damage is caused by forklift impact. Operators clip uprights during turning, catch beams while raising or lowering forks, and sideswipe frames while traveling through aisles. Every impact event — no matter how minor it appears — must be reported, inspected, and documented. Column protectors reduce but do not eliminate damage.

**Load capacity.** Every pallet rack section must have posted load capacity signage. Overloading racks is a leading cause of rack collapse. If you do not know the rated capacity of your racking, hire an engineer to calculate it before someone gets killed by the calculation you never made.

### Ergonomic Lifting

Musculoskeletal injuries from manual lifting are the most common injury in warehousing by volume. Back injuries, shoulder injuries, knee injuries, and repetitive strain injuries account for more lost workdays than any other injury category.

**Risk assessment.** Evaluate every manual lifting task in your operation: weight, frequency, height, reach distance, twisting, duration. Identify which tasks exceed ergonomic guidelines (NIOSH recommends a maximum recommended weight limit of 51 pounds under ideal conditions — and warehouse conditions are rarely ideal).

**Engineering controls.** Adjustable-height workstations, conveyor systems, vacuum lifters, pallet positioners, and ergonomic packaging that reduces individual package weight. The cheapest back injury claim costs more than most ergonomic equipment.

**Administrative controls.** Job rotation to distribute lifting demands across different muscle groups. Team lifting procedures for loads that exceed individual capability. Mandatory rest breaks during sustained manual handling operations.

**Training.** Proper lifting technique, recognition of early symptoms (fatigue, soreness, numbness, tingling), and the reporting procedure for ergonomic concerns. Training alone does not prevent ergonomic injuries — but training combined with engineering controls and administrative controls reduces them significantly.

## Indoor Heat Illness Prevention

California's indoor heat standard applies to warehouse operations when the temperature inside reaches 82 degrees Fahrenheit. For warehouses without climate control — which is most warehouses — this threshold is exceeded from May through October and potentially year-round near heat-generating equipment.

**At 82 degrees:**
- Provide fresh, cool drinking water
- Provide access to cool-down areas
- Implement acclimatization procedures for new employees
- Train employees on heat illness symptoms and first aid

**At 87 degrees:**
- Measure and record temperatures
- Implement engineering controls (ventilation, fans, cooling systems, reflective barriers)
- Implement administrative controls (schedule intensive work for cooler hours, increase rest breaks)

**At 95 degrees:**
- Implement high heat procedures
- Observe employees for symptoms
- Establish a buddy system
- Ensure effective communication for emergency response

Monitor dock areas independently — they can be significantly hotter than the warehouse interior due to sun exposure and engine exhaust from idling trucks.
