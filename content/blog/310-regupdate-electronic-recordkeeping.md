# OSHA Electronic Recordkeeping Submission Rule Expanded

OSHA wants your injury data, and they want it electronically. The expanded electronic recordkeeping submission rule dramatically increases the number of employers required to submit detailed injury and illness records through OSHA's online portal.

## What Changed

The final rule expands electronic submission requirements under 29 CFR Part 1904:

**Previous requirement:** Establishments with 250 or more employees submitted Form 300A (Annual Summary) electronically.

**New requirement:** Establishments with 100 or more employees in designated high-hazard industries must now submit:

- Form 300 (Log of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses)
- Form 301 (Injury and Illness Incident Report)
- Form 300A (Annual Summary)

This is a fundamental shift. OSHA is no longer just collecting summary data. They are collecting case-specific incident details — including descriptions of how injuries occurred, body parts affected, and whether the incidents resulted in days away, restricted work, or job transfer.

## Who Is Affected

Establishments with 100 or more employees in industries classified as high-hazard by OSHA. These industries include but are not limited to:

- Manufacturing (all sectors)
- Construction
- Agriculture
- Transportation and warehousing
- Healthcare
- Utilities
- Wholesale trade

Establishments with 20 to 249 employees in these industries must continue to submit Form 300A annually.

## What Employers Must Do

1. **Verify your submission obligations.** Check OSHA's updated list of designated high-hazard industries at osha.gov/recordkeeping. If your NAICS code is on the list and you have 100 or more employees, you must submit Forms 300, 301, and 300A.

2. **Audit your OSHA 300 Logs immediately.** The data you submit will be used by OSHA for targeting inspections. Inaccurate or incomplete entries are themselves citable violations — and they may also trigger an inspection if they reveal patterns.

3. **Register on OSHA's Injury Tracking Application (ITA).** Submissions are made through osha.gov/injuryreporting. Ensure your establishment is registered and your authorized representative has access.

4. **Scrub for accuracy before submission.** Verify that each entry on your 300 Log includes accurate classification (death, days away, restricted work, or other recordable), correct job title, and a complete description of the incident.

5. **Submit by the deadline.** Electronic submissions for the prior calendar year are due by March 2 annually.

## Effective Date

The expanded rule applies to calendar year 2026 data, with the first expanded submissions due March 2, 2027.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Failure to submit required electronic records carries penalties of up to $16,550 per violation. Failure to maintain accurate OSHA 300 Logs — which is what OSHA will discover when they review your submissions — carries additional penalties up to $16,550 per inaccurate entry.

Your injury data is no longer just an internal document. It is a federal submission. Treat it accordingly.
