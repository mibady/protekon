This is one of the most misunderstood areas of workplace safety regulation, and getting it wrong can cost you real money.

**The three categories:**

1. **Federal OSHA states (22 states).** Federal OSHA directly enforces workplace safety standards. The federal agency conducts inspections, issues citations, and collects penalties.

2. **State plan states (25 states + DC and territories).** These states operate their own occupational safety programs that have been approved by federal OSHA. California is a state plan state -- Cal/OSHA is the enforcing agency, not federal OSHA. State plans must be "at least as effective" as federal OSHA, and many states -- California especially -- go well beyond the federal minimums.

3. **Hybrid states (4 states).** These states cover only public sector employees under their state plan. Private sector employers in hybrid states fall under federal OSHA.

**What "at least as effective" means in practice:**

State plan states can adopt standards that are stricter than federal OSHA. California does this routinely. SB 553 is a perfect example -- there is no equivalent federal workplace violence prevention standard for general industry. California created its own, and it applies on top of all federal requirements.

**Multi-state employers: comply per worksite.**

If you have operations in California and Texas, your California worksites follow Cal/OSHA rules and your Texas worksites follow federal OSHA rules. You do not get to pick the easier standard. The law that governs is determined by where the work is performed, not where the company is headquartered.

**The bottom line for California employers:**

You answer to Cal/OSHA, and Cal/OSHA is more aggressive than federal OSHA in both standards and enforcement. Plan accordingly.
