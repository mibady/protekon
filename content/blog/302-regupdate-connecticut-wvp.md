# Connecticut Workplace Violence Prevention Act Effective

California is no longer alone. Connecticut has enacted its own workplace violence prevention law, and if you operate in healthcare, social services, or education in the Constitution State, your compliance clock is ticking.

## What Changed

Connecticut Governor signed HB 6931 into law, establishing mandatory workplace violence prevention requirements for employers in designated high-risk industries. The law requires covered employers to develop and implement written workplace violence prevention plans, conduct hazard assessments, provide annual employee training, and maintain incident logs.

This is not a watered-down suggestion. It is a mandate with enforcement teeth and penalties for non-compliance.

## Who Is Affected

The Connecticut WVP Act applies to employers in:

- **Healthcare facilities** — hospitals, outpatient clinics, urgent care, behavioral health, long-term care
- **Social service agencies** — child welfare, family services, homeless services, substance abuse treatment
- **Educational institutions** — K-12 schools, colleges, universities (both public and private)

Employers with 50 or more employees in these sectors must comply with the full plan and training requirements. Employers with 10-49 employees have a simplified compliance pathway but are still covered.

## What Employers Must Do

1. **Conduct a workplace violence hazard assessment.** Walk your facilities. Identify risk factors — isolated work areas, cash handling, volatile client populations, inadequate lighting, poor sightlines. Document everything.

2. **Develop a written Workplace Violence Prevention Plan.** The plan must include: hazard assessment findings, control measures, reporting procedures, incident response protocols, and designated responsible persons.

3. **Provide annual training.** All employees must receive workplace violence prevention training within 90 days of hire and annually thereafter. Training must cover your specific plan, de-escalation techniques, reporting procedures, and emergency protocols.

4. **Establish an incident reporting system.** Employees must have a clear, accessible method to report threats, intimidation, and physical violence. Retaliation against reporters is explicitly prohibited.

5. **Maintain records for five years.** Training records, incident reports, and hazard assessments must be retained for a minimum of five years.

## Effective Date

July 1, 2026. Employers have until October 1, 2026 to complete initial employee training.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

The Connecticut Department of Labor will enforce the Act through inspections and complaint investigations. Penalties range from $5,000 to $10,000 per violation for first offenses, escalating to $10,000 to $25,000 for repeat violations. Willful violations carry additional penalties.

If you operate in multiple states, the compliance landscape is expanding. Build your WVP infrastructure now so it scales.
