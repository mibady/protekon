# FAQ: How do I protect employee PII in incident logs?

You are legally required to maintain incident records. You are also legally required to protect employee privacy. These obligations coexist, and handling them poorly creates liability on both sides.

Here is how to get it right:

**Redact names in shared versions.**
Your OSHA 300 Log is posted annually where employees can see it. The annual summary (300A) must not include employee names. If you share investigation reports with committees, insurance carriers, or consultants, redact personally identifiable information from those copies.

**Limit access to the investigation team.**
Full incident reports with names, medical details, and witness statements should be accessible only to those who need them: the safety manager, HR, legal counsel, and the direct investigation team. Not the entire management chain. Not the break room bulletin board.

**Store records securely.**
Physical records go in locked filing cabinets. Digital records go in encrypted, access-controlled systems. Not a shared Google Drive folder that half the company can browse.

**Never include PII in annual summaries.**
The OSHA 300A summary that you post from February 1 through April 30 must contain aggregate data only. No names. No identifying details. If someone can figure out who was involved from the summary, you have disclosed too much.

**Violent incident logs require extra care.**
Under the WVPP, if the perpetrator is an employee, you record a description but do not name them in the log. Protect both the victim and the accused from unnecessary exposure.

**The practical rule:** Maintain two versions of every incident record. The complete version stays locked down with restricted access. The shared version is redacted to remove anything that identifies a specific individual.

Privacy failures erode trust. Employees who fear their medical information will be shared openly are employees who stop reporting injuries. And unreported injuries are the ones that become catastrophic.

Protect the data. Protect the people.
