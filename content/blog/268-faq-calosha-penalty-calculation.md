title: "FAQ: How does Cal/OSHA calculate penalties?"
faqCategory: cal-osha
excerpt: "Cal/OSHA calculates penalties using a gravity-based formula: severity times probability times extent, then adjusted for company size, good faith, and violation history."
---

Cal/OSHA penalties are not arbitrary. They follow a structured calculation that every employer should understand because the math determines whether you pay $5,000 or $25,000 for the same type of violation.

**The gravity-based formula:**

Penalties start with three factors multiplied together:

1. **Severity.** How bad could the injury or illness be? Minor first aid vs. hospitalization vs. fatality. Higher potential severity means a higher base penalty.

2. **Probability.** How likely is it that the hazard will actually cause harm? A machine with an exposed blade running eight hours a day has high probability. A rarely accessed storage room with a minor trip hazard has low probability.

3. **Extent.** How many employees are exposed to the hazard? One worker or fifty? More exposed employees means a higher base penalty.

These three factors produce the gravity of the violation, which sets the initial penalty amount.

**Adjustment factors:**

The base penalty is then adjusted up or down based on:

- **Employer size.** Smaller employers (fewer employees) receive reductions. This is not an exemption -- you still pay, just less. Large employers get no size reduction.

- **Good faith.** Do you have a functioning safety program? Have you made genuine efforts to comply? Employers with documented safety programs, training records, and hazard assessments may receive reductions. Employers with nothing get no adjustment.

- **History.** Have you been cited before? A clean history helps. Prior citations -- especially for similar hazards -- eliminate reductions and can trigger repeat classification with enhanced penalties.

**The practical takeaway:**

You control two of the three adjustment factors. Maintain a documented safety program (good faith) and fix cited hazards promptly (history). These are the levers that reduce your penalty exposure before an inspector ever walks through your door.

The cheapest penalty is the one you never receive.
