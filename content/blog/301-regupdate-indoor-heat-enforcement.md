# Cal/OSHA Indoor Heat Illness Standard Enforcement Begins

California's indoor heat illness prevention standard is no longer in the education-and-outreach phase. Enforcement has begun. If your indoor workplace hits 82 degrees Fahrenheit, you need a written plan, access to water, cool-down areas, and training documentation. Today.

## What Changed

Cal/OSHA has transitioned the indoor heat illness prevention standard (8 CCR Section 3396) from advisory guidance to active enforcement. Inspectors are now issuing citations for non-compliance during both programmed inspections and complaint-driven investigations.

The standard applies when indoor temperatures reach 82 degrees for general provisions and 87 degrees for high-heat procedures. Engineering controls, administrative controls, cool-down rest areas, and acclimatization procedures are all mandatory — not suggested.

## Who Is Affected

Any California employer with indoor work areas where temperatures reach or exceed 82 degrees. High-risk industries include:

- Warehousing and distribution centers
- Manufacturing and fabrication
- Commercial kitchens and restaurants
- Laundry and dry-cleaning facilities
- Food processing plants
- Indoor agriculture operations

## What Employers Must Do

1. **Develop a written Heat Illness Prevention Plan for indoor environments.** This is separate from your outdoor heat plan. It must include monitoring procedures, control measures, emergency response protocols, and acclimatization schedules.

2. **Install temperature monitoring.** You cannot manage what you do not measure. Deploy thermometers or temperature logging devices in all work areas subject to heat exposure.

3. **Provide cool-down areas.** When temperatures hit 82 degrees, employees must have access to a cool-down area maintained below 82 degrees. This area must be available at all times — not just during designated breaks.

4. **Provide drinking water.** Fresh, pure, suitably cool water must be available free of charge and placed as close as practicable to work areas. One quart per employee per hour is the minimum standard.

5. **Train every employee and supervisor.** Training must cover heat illness symptoms, prevention measures, emergency response, and the employer's specific written plan. Document everything.

## Effective Date

Enforcement is active now. Citations are being issued.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

General violations up to $18,000 per instance. Serious violations — and any heat-related hospitalization or fatality will be classified as serious — up to $25,000 per instance. Repeat or willful violations up to $156,259.

Heat illness is preventable. Heat illness citations are also preventable. Choose prevention.
