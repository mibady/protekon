# FAQ: What formats are acceptable for compliance training?

Here's the straight answer: California accepts multiple training formats, but not all formats work for all topics. Know the difference or pay the price.

**Acceptable formats include:**

- **Classroom instruction** — traditional lecture with Q&A. Still the gold standard for complex topics.
- **Hands-on demonstrations** — required for certain hazards. You cannot teach someone to use a respirator with a PowerPoint slide.
- **Online/computer-based training** — acceptable for many topics, but must be interactive. A 45-minute video your employees click through while checking their phones does not count.
- **Toolbox talks** — short, focused safety meetings at the job site. Great for reinforcement and seasonal topics like heat illness.
- **Video-based training** — acceptable as a supplement, but rarely sufficient as the sole method.

**The critical caveat:** Some topics legally require hands-on, practical training. Forklift certification demands observed performance evaluations. Respirator fit testing requires physical demonstration. Fall protection needs practice with actual equipment.

Cal/OSHA does not care how slick your training platform looks. They care whether your employees actually understood the material and can apply it.

**One more thing most employers get wrong:** All training must be delivered in a language the employee understands. If half your crew speaks Spanish, your English-only online module is worthless — and citable.

The format matters less than the outcome. Can your employee identify the hazard, explain the procedure, and demonstrate the safe practice? If yes, your training format works. If no, it doesn't matter how much you spent on the e-learning platform.

Pick the format that actually teaches. Document everything.
