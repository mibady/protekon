# Maryland WVP Legislation Advances

The workplace violence prevention wave is expanding beyond California and Connecticut. Maryland's legislature has advanced HB 1247, a comprehensive workplace violence prevention bill targeting healthcare, social services, and retail employers. Smart employers are preparing now.

## What Changed

The Maryland General Assembly has passed HB 1247 through committee and scheduled it for a full floor vote. The bill would require covered employers to:

- Conduct workplace violence hazard assessments
- Develop and implement written workplace violence prevention plans
- Provide annual employee training on violence prevention and de-escalation
- Establish incident reporting and investigation procedures
- Maintain records for a minimum of five years

The bill draws heavily from California's SB 553 framework but includes Maryland-specific provisions around healthcare worker protections and retail establishment security requirements.

## Who Is Affected

If enacted as drafted, the law would apply to:

- Healthcare facilities with 25 or more employees
- Social service agencies with 10 or more employees
- Retail establishments with 50 or more employees

State and local government employers in these sectors would also be covered.

## What Employers Must Do

Even though the bill has not yet been signed into law, prudent employers should begin preparing now:

1. **Conduct a baseline hazard assessment.** Walk your facilities and identify risk factors for workplace violence — public-facing work areas, cash handling, isolated work locations, volatile populations, inadequate security measures.

2. **Draft a Workplace Violence Prevention Plan.** Use the California SB 553 framework as a template. Plans that meet California's standard will almost certainly meet or exceed Maryland's requirements.

3. **Inventory your current training programs.** Determine whether your existing employee training covers workplace violence recognition, de-escalation, and reporting. Identify gaps that will need to be filled.

4. **Establish an incident tracking system.** Begin logging workplace violence incidents now. When the law takes effect, you will need a mature system — not one you built overnight.

5. **Monitor the legislative process.** Track HB 1247 through the Maryland General Assembly. Engage your industry associations to provide input during the amendment process.

## Effective Date

If passed as drafted, the law would take effect January 1, 2027, with a 180-day compliance window for covered employers.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

The draft bill includes civil penalties of $1,000 to $7,000 per violation for first offenses, escalating to $7,000 to $25,000 for repeat violations within three years. The Maryland Occupational Safety and Health division would have enforcement authority.

Multi-state employers: the trend is clear. Build a scalable WVP framework now. Retrofitting state-by-state is expensive and inefficient.
