A HIPAA breach is any unauthorized acquisition, access, use, or disclosure of protected health information that compromises the security or privacy of that information. The reporting obligations depend on the size and scope of the breach.

**What constitutes a breach:**

Any impermissible use or disclosure of PHI is presumed to be a breach unless you can demonstrate through a risk assessment that there is a low probability the PHI was compromised. The burden of proof is on you to demonstrate it was NOT a breach, not on HHS to prove it was.

The four-factor risk assessment considers:
1. The nature and extent of the PHI involved
2. Who received or accessed the PHI
3. Whether the PHI was actually acquired or viewed
4. The extent to which the risk has been mitigated

**Reporting timelines:**

**Notify affected individuals: within 60 days** of discovering the breach. Written notification by first-class mail or email (if the individual consented to electronic communication). Must include what happened, what PHI was involved, steps individuals should take, and what you are doing to investigate and prevent recurrence.

**Breaches affecting 500 or more individuals:**
- Notify HHS immediately (within 60 days)
- Notify prominent media outlets in the state or jurisdiction

**Breaches affecting fewer than 500 individuals:**
- Report to HHS annually, no later than 60 days after the end of the calendar year in which the breach was discovered

**The wall of shame:**

HHS maintains a public, searchable database of all breaches affecting 500 or more individuals. Your company name, the number of individuals affected, and the type of breach are displayed publicly. This is reputational damage that no amount of PR can undo.

**The practical imperative:**

Have a breach response plan before you need it. When a breach occurs, the 60-day clock starts ticking immediately. Scrambling to figure out your obligations while the deadline approaches is a recipe for additional violations.
