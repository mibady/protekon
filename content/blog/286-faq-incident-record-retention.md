# FAQ: How long must incident investigation records be retained?

Different records have different retention periods. Throw something away too early and you have destroyed evidence. Here are the rules.

**OSHA 300 Log (Log of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses):**
5 years following the end of the calendar year the records cover. If the 300 Log covers 2025, you keep it through December 31, 2030. And you must update it during that period if new information surfaces.

**Violent Incident Log (WVPP requirement):**
5 years. California's Workplace Violence Prevention Plan requires a violent incident log with specific data fields. Retain each entry for a minimum of 5 years from the date of the incident.

**Investigation reports:**
No single Cal/OSHA regulation specifies a retention period for internal investigation reports, but the best practice — and the one that protects you legally — is 5 years minimum. Match the retention period of the 300 Log so everything aligns.

**Exposure records (chemical, noise, biological):**
30 years. This is the one that catches employers off guard. If employees were exposed to hazardous substances or harmful physical agents, those exposure records must be retained for 30 years beyond the duration of employment. This includes monitoring data, medical surveillance, and material safety data sheets.

**Employee medical records:**
30 years beyond the duration of employment. Same as exposure records under 8 CCR 3204.

**The practical approach:**

Keep everything for at least 5 years. Keep anything involving chemical, biological, or physical exposure for 30 years. When in doubt, keep it longer.

Storage is cheap. Litigation is expensive. A record you destroyed last year is the record opposing counsel will request first.

Build a retention schedule. Automate destruction dates. Never destroy anything during active litigation or an open investigation.

Keep the records. They are your defense.
