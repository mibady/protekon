# Cal/OSHA Heat Illness Enforcement Campaign Announced

Summer is coming. And so is Cal/OSHA.

The Division of Occupational Safety and Health has announced its 2026 Heat Illness Prevention Enforcement Campaign — the most aggressive in the program's history. If you have outdoor workers in California, you need to be ready before May 1.

## What Changed

Cal/OSHA's 2026 campaign expands on prior years with three significant changes:

1. **Earlier start date.** Enforcement begins May 1, two weeks earlier than previous campaigns, reflecting California's pattern of earlier-onset heat events.

2. **Increased inspection targets.** Cal/OSHA has committed to a 25% increase in heat-related inspections compared to 2025, with dedicated enforcement teams in the Central Valley, Inland Empire, and Southern California desert regions.

3. **Focus on high-heat procedures.** Inspections will specifically verify that employers have implemented enhanced procedures when temperatures exceed 95 degrees, including mandatory buddy systems, pre-shift meetings, and close observation of new employees during acclimatization.

## Who Is Affected

All California employers with employees performing outdoor work, with priority enforcement targeting:

- Agriculture and farm labor
- Construction
- Landscaping and grounds maintenance
- Warehousing with significant outdoor loading and unloading
- Road work and utility line work
- Oil and gas extraction

## What Employers Must Do

1. **Review and update your written Heat Illness Prevention Plan.** It must address water provision, shade access, acclimatization procedures, high-heat procedures (95 degrees and above), emergency response, and training.

2. **Ensure water, shade, and rest are available from day one of the campaign.** Water must be fresh, pure, suitably cool, and provided free of charge — one quart per employee per hour for the entire shift. Shade must be available when temperatures exceed 80 degrees.

3. **Implement acclimatization procedures.** New employees and employees returning from absence must be closely observed for 14 days. This is not optional — it is a regulatory requirement that Cal/OSHA inspectors specifically verify.

4. **Train all employees and supervisors before May 1.** Training must cover the employer's specific plan, signs and symptoms of heat illness, emergency response procedures, and employee rights to request shade and rest without retaliation.

5. **Post the Cal/OSHA heat illness prevention poster.** Available in English, Spanish, and other languages from the Cal/OSHA website.

## Effective Date

Campaign enforcement begins May 1, 2026. The underlying standard (8 CCR 3395) is enforceable year-round.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Heat illness fatalities trigger mandatory serious citations starting at $25,000. Willful violations reach $156,259. Cal/OSHA has successfully pursued criminal referrals for heat-related deaths where employers failed to provide basic water, shade, and rest.

Water is cheap. Shade is cheap. Funerals are not.
