# FAQ: What goes in the violent incident log?

The violent incident log is a separate recordkeeping requirement under California's WVPP. It is not the same as your OSHA 300 Log. It captures specific data about workplace violence events that Cal/OSHA can request during any inspection.

**Required data fields for each entry:**

- **Date, time, and location** of the incident — specific enough to identify the exact area within your facility
- **Detailed description** of the incident — what happened, in narrative form
- **Type classification** — Type 1 (criminal intent), Type 2 (customer/client), Type 3 (worker-on-worker), or Type 4 (personal relationship)
- **Violence type** — threat, physical assault, intimidation, verbal abuse, or other aggressive behavior
- **Weapons involved** — if any weapons were present, displayed, or used
- **Consequences** — injuries sustained, medical treatment provided, work time lost
- **Perpetrator description** — physical description and relationship to the workplace. If the perpetrator is an employee, do not record their name in the log. Use a description only.
- **Circumstances** — what led up to the incident, contributing factors, environmental conditions
- **Staffing information** — staffing levels at the time of the incident

**Retention:** 5 years minimum from the date of the incident.

**PII protection:** This is critical. The log must be maintained in a way that protects the privacy of both victims and perpetrators. Employee names should not appear in the log if it could be accessed by unauthorized individuals. Use descriptions, not names, for employee perpetrators.

**The common mistake:** Employers either do not maintain this log at all — because they did not know it was required — or they conflate it with the OSHA 300 Log. They are different documents with different requirements.

Every workplace violence event gets logged. Every entry includes the required fields. No exceptions.
