title: "FAQ: What are OSHA whistleblower protections?"

Whistleblower protections exist at both federal and state levels, and they have real teeth. Employers who retaliate against employees for raising safety concerns are writing a check they do not want to cash.

**Federal protection: OSHA Section 11(c).**

It is illegal to retaliate against any employee who:
- Files a safety or health complaint
- Participates in an OSHA inspection
- Reports a work-related injury or illness
- Exercises any right under the OSH Act

Retaliation includes termination, demotion, transfer, reduction in hours, intimidation, threats, reassignment to undesirable work, or any other adverse action taken because the employee exercised their rights.

**California protection: Labor Code Section 6310.**

California provides additional protections that are broader than federal law. No employer may discharge, discipline, or discriminate against any employee who:
- Makes any oral or written complaint about employee safety or health to any government agency, their employer, or any other person
- Participates in an occupational health and safety committee
- Institutes any proceeding related to employee safety

**Filing a retaliation complaint:**

- **Federal:** File with OSHA within 30 days of the retaliatory action. This deadline is firm.
- **California:** File with the Labor Commissioner. California provides longer filing windows than federal law in many cases.

**What employers get wrong:**

They think timing provides cover. Fire someone two months after a complaint and claim it was "performance-related." OSHA and Cal/OSHA investigators are trained to see through this. Temporal proximity between a complaint and an adverse action creates a presumption of retaliation that is difficult to overcome.

**The smart employer's approach:**

Create a culture where employees report concerns freely. An internal report you fix today prevents the Cal/OSHA complaint you cannot control tomorrow.
