# OSHA Silica Enforcement Expansion

OSHA's respirable crystalline silica standard has been on the books since 2016 for construction and 2018 for general industry. But enforcement just got significantly more aggressive. If you work with stone, concrete, brick, or any silica-containing material, pay attention.

## What Changed

OSHA has expanded its National Emphasis Program (NEP) for respirable crystalline silica to include additional target industries and increased inspection frequency. The expansion specifically adds engineered stone fabrication, concrete product manufacturing, and foundry operations to the priority inspection list.

The agency has also lowered the threshold for triggering programmed inspections. Establishments previously below the radar are now in scope.

## Who Is Affected

- Construction contractors performing cutting, grinding, drilling, or demolishing concrete, stone, or masonry
- Engineered stone (quartz) countertop fabricators and installers
- Concrete product manufacturers
- Foundries and casting operations
- Glass manufacturers
- Hydraulic fracturing operations

## What Employers Must Do

1. **Verify your exposure assessment is current.** If you have not conducted air monitoring within the last 12 months, or if work conditions have changed, you need a new assessment. The permissible exposure limit is 50 micrograms per cubic meter as an 8-hour TWA.

2. **Implement Table 1 controls or equivalent.** Construction employers can follow the specified engineering controls in OSHA's Table 1 for 18 common tasks. General industry employers must implement feasible engineering and work practice controls.

3. **Offer medical surveillance.** Employees exposed at or above the action level (25 micrograms per cubic meter) for 30 or more days per year must be offered medical exams within 30 days of initial assignment and every three years thereafter.

4. **Maintain a written exposure control plan.** The plan must describe tasks involving silica exposure, engineering controls used, housekeeping procedures, and restricted access protocols.

5. **Train employees on silica hazards.** Training must cover health effects of silica exposure, specific tasks that create exposure, engineering controls in use, and the purpose and use of respiratory protection.

## Effective Date

The expanded NEP is active now. OSHA area offices are conducting programmed inspections under the revised targeting criteria.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Standard OSHA penalty structure applies: up to $16,550 for serious violations and $165,514 for willful or repeat violations. But silica violations frequently involve multiple citation items — exposure control, medical surveillance, training, and housekeeping — which compound quickly. A single inspection can produce six-figure penalties.

Silicosis is irreversible. Compliance is not.
