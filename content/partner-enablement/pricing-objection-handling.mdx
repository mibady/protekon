# Pricing Objection Handling

Every objection below is a pricing objection wearing a different coat. The response is never to discount. The response is to reframe the comparison, re-anchor the value, and — where it makes sense — walk away.

The one rule underneath all of these: **you never commodity-price this.** If the prospect wants compliance for the price of a poster subscription, that prospect is not your customer. Thank them, send them to the poster vendor, and move on. The minute you discount to keep a deal, you train the entire referral network to ask for the same discount.

## 1. "That's more than I expected."

This is not an objection. It is an invitation to anchor.

> "That's a fair reaction. Most operators expect a number closer to what a compliance poster subscription costs — around $30 a month. This isn't that. This is an ongoing AI Compliance Officer who drafts the plans, tracks the regulations, logs the incidents, and produces the audit packages. If you priced the attorney and consultant hours you'd otherwise spend on this, it's a fraction of what you'd pay piecemeal. The number is what it is because the work is what it is."

Do not move. Do not offer 10% off. Sit with the silence.

## 2. "I can get compliance software for $99 a month."

Yes, they can. That is not what you are selling.

> "You absolutely can. Those tools are self-serve libraries — you log in, you download a template, you fill it out, you keep it current. If you've got the bandwidth and the expertise to run that internally, that is a real option. What we do is the opposite: you don't log in, you don't fill it out, you don't keep it current. I do. And when OSHA or the DOL shows up, the audit packet is already built. The $99 tool puts the work on you. This puts the work on us."

If they still want the $99 tool, they should buy it. You are not the fit. Move on.

## 3. "Can you do $397 a month instead?"

No. And here is the script:

> "I can't, and I want to tell you why rather than haggle. The Essentials tier at $597 is already priced to deliver the service profitably. If I drop it to $397, I either cut the scope — fewer documents, no regulatory monitoring, no monthly review — or I lose money on your account and the service quality slips within a quarter. Neither helps you. What I can do is scope the engagement more tightly: if you don't need the full library on day one, we can start with the three highest-risk documents, still at $597, and expand scope without raising price as your needs grow."

You have offered a yes (tighter initial scope) without moving price. Nine times out of ten the prospect accepts. The tenth time, they were never closing anyway.

## 4. "My attorney already handles this."

Good. Attorneys are expensive and reactive.

> "Attorneys are who you call when something has already gone wrong. What I do is what keeps you out of the attorney's office. I maintain the documents, track the regulatory changes, and produce the audit trail. Your attorney handles the 10% of situations that escalate. I handle the 90% that never should have escalated in the first place. Most of my clients keep their attorney and add this — the two don't compete."

Never badmouth the attorney. Reframe the scope.

## 5. "I'll think about it."

Rarely true. Usually means one of: price fear, decision-making authority uncertainty, or a competing priority.

> "Happy to give you time. Before I go — is the hesitation about the number, the scope, or the timing? I'd rather answer the real question now than have you circle back in three weeks still wondering."

Wait. Let them name the real objection. Then handle that objection using the scripts above.

## 6. "We already passed our last audit. We're fine."

They are not fine. They are lucky.

> "Glad you passed. Audits are sample-based — the agencies cycle industries and jurisdictions. The last audit being clean tells us nothing about the next one, especially as SB 553, HIPAA, and OSHA keep adding requirements. What I offer isn't an audit response. It's an audit-ready state. The difference is when the next letter arrives, you already have the answer — you don't spend two weeks building one."

Follow with: "What did your last audit cost you in staff hours to prepare for?" Use their answer as the anchor.

## 7. "I need to check with my partner / CFO / spouse."

Let them. Give them a tool to do it with.

> "Of course. Let me send you a one-page summary that covers the scope, the investment, and the first 90 days. That way whoever you're running it past sees the same thing you see. When do you expect to have the conversation — I'll schedule a follow-up for the day after so we don't lose momentum."

The follow-up is scheduled on the call. Not an open-ended "I'll check back next week." A specific day.

## 8. "Can I pay quarterly instead of monthly?"

Yes — at a small premium, not a discount.

> "We can do quarterly. Quarterly is $1,811 for Essentials, $2,721 for Professional, $3,941 for Enterprise — that's the monthly rate with no reduction. If you want quarterly to smooth your billing, that works. If you're hoping for a quarterly discount, we don't do that: the price is the price, whether you pay it monthly, quarterly, or annually."

Same logic applies to annual prepay. You do not discount for prepay. You offer the convenience of the term without trading margin for it.

## 9. "What if I want to cancel?"

Never hide this answer. Say it up front in the proposal and repeat it here.

> "Month-to-month. Thirty-day written notice. No cancellation fee. I would rather you cancel cleanly than stay unhappy. What I will ask is a single 15-minute exit call so I understand what didn't work — that call makes my service better for the next client."

Low-friction cancellation is a feature, not a bug. It tells the prospect you are confident you will earn the renewal every month. Most clients stay past 24 months once they settle in.

## 10. "Your competitor said they'd do it for $299."

Let them go.

> "They might. If $299 is what you're solving for, you should take that deal. I'd just ask one question — do they draft the documents for you, or do they give you a template library you fill in yourself? If it's the template library, that's a different product. If it's the drafting service, ask them how they do it at that price and make sure the answer isn't 'we outsource the drafting to someone in a different time zone and hope for the best.' Some of those operations are fine. Some aren't. Either way, at our price we're doing the work ourselves, and you know who to call when something matters."

You are allowed to lose this deal. You are not allowed to match the price.

## The non-negotiable

Every partner has one deal, usually in their first 90 days, where they are tempted to discount to prove momentum to themselves. Do not.

The cost of the first discounted client is not the lost margin on that client. It is the precedent you set in your own head — that the price is negotiable. Once that precedent is set, it follows you through every proposal for the rest of the year. Every subsequent prospect senses the flexibility and asks for it.

Hold the line on the first ten deals. By deal eleven you will stop feeling the urge. That is when the practice starts to work.
