# SB 553 — What Every Employee Should Know

## What is SB 553?

SB 553 is a California law. It went into full effect on **July 1, 2024**. It requires almost every employer in California to have a **Workplace Violence Prevention Plan (WVPP)**. You should have received a copy — or know where to find it.

This handout explains what the law does, what your rights are, and what you are expected to do.

## Why this law exists

Workplace violence is a leading cause of injury at work. SB 553 was written to:

- Make sure every workplace has a **written plan** to stop violence before it happens.
- Give workers a clear, **safe way to report** threats and incidents without fear of being punished.
- Require employers to **train you** on how to spot and respond to dangerous situations.
- Require employers to **keep a log** of every incident — with your personal details kept private.

## The four types of workplace violence

The law recognizes four types. Your employer's plan covers all of them.

- **Type 1 — Criminal Intent.** A stranger commits a crime (robbery, assault) with no legitimate business at your workplace.
- **Type 2 — Customer or Client.** Someone you serve (customer, patient, student, visitor) becomes violent or threatening.
- **Type 3 — Worker-on-Worker.** A current or former coworker threatens or attacks another worker.
- **Type 4 — Personal Relationship.** Someone with a personal connection (ex-partner, family) brings violence into the workplace.

## Your rights under SB 553

You have the right to:

- **See the plan.** Ask your supervisor for a copy of your employer's Workplace Violence Prevention Plan.
- **Report concerns without retaliation.** The law protects you. You cannot be fired, demoted, or disciplined for reporting a threat or incident in good faith.
- **Be trained.** You must receive training when you are hired, once a year after that, and any time the plan changes.
- **Be involved.** You can give feedback on the plan and suggest improvements.
- **Have your privacy protected.** The incident log keeps personal details out. No names, no medical info.

## Your responsibilities

- **Report all incidents and threats** — even verbal threats, even if no one was hurt.
- **Follow the procedures** in your employer's plan. Know the emergency exits, lockdown steps, and how to call for help.
- **Attend the training.** It is paid work time. You cannot be required to attend on your own time without pay.
- **Use the reporting channels.** Talk to your supervisor, use the hotline, or use any anonymous system your employer provides.
- **Do not retaliate** against coworkers who report incidents.

## How to report an incident

Use any of these paths — whichever feels safest to you:

1. Tell your direct supervisor right away.
2. Use your employer's anonymous hotline or online reporting form.
3. Contact HR or the safety coordinator named in the plan.
4. Call 911 if the threat is immediate.
5. Contact Cal/OSHA directly if you believe your employer is not following the law.

You do **not** have to use the chain of command if you feel unsafe doing so.

## What happens after you report

- The incident is logged in the **Violent Incident Log**. Your name is kept out.
- A supervisor or safety coordinator investigates within a short time window.
- The employer must take corrective action — new controls, extra training, a fix to the physical space, or whatever the situation needs.
- You should be told how the matter was resolved unless doing so would violate someone else's privacy.
- You will not be punished for making a good-faith report.

## What your employer must do

- Have a written WVPP that is specific to your workplace.
- Train every worker when hired, once a year, and whenever the plan changes.
- Keep a **Violent Incident Log** with no personal details, for at least **5 years**.
- Investigate every reported incident.
- Review the plan at least once a year and after any incident.
- Make the plan available to you, Cal/OSHA, and your representatives on request.

## Common questions

**Q: Do I have to put my name on the log?**
No. The Violent Incident Log keeps names out by law. It records what happened, when, and where — not who.

**Q: What if I see something but am not sure it is a threat?**
Report it anyway. Small signals matter. Trained reviewers decide what is and is not a threat.

**Q: What if my supervisor is the problem?**
Use the anonymous hotline, go to HR, or contact Cal/OSHA directly. You are protected from retaliation.

**Q: Does this apply to remote workers?**
Yes. The plan covers anywhere you perform work for your employer, including home offices and field sites.

## Who to contact

- **Your supervisor or safety coordinator** (named in the plan)
- **HR department**
- **Anonymous hotline** (listed in your plan)
- **Cal/OSHA** — 1-833-579-0927 or dir.ca.gov/dosh
- **911** — for any immediate danger
