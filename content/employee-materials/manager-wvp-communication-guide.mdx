# Manager Guide — Communicating the WVP Plan to Your Team

You are the front line of your Workplace Violence Prevention Plan (WVPP). Your team will follow the plan as well as you explain it. This guide gives you the talking points, the answers to common questions, and the signals that should make you escalate.

## Your role in one sentence

You are the person who makes the plan real — by rolling it out, running the training, closing the loop on reports, and modeling that "if you see something, say something" is safe here.

## Five talking points for the team meeting

Use these when you introduce or refresh the plan. Say them in order, in your own words.

1. **Why this plan exists.** California law (SB 553) requires it, but the real reason is that your team deserves to come home safely. The plan protects every person in this building — including you.

2. **What is covered.** Four types of workplace violence: criminal strangers, upset customers or clients, coworker-on-coworker conflict, and personal relationships that follow someone to work. The plan covers all four.

3. **How to report.** Name every reporting path you offer — supervisor, HR, anonymous hotline, safety coordinator. Emphasize: **there is no wrong path**, and **no one gets punished for reporting**. That is the law, not a company policy.

4. **What happens after a report.** Investigation within days. Incident logged with no names. Corrective action — new training, new controls, fixed hardware, whatever the situation needs. The reporter is told how it was resolved whenever possible.

5. **What we expect from you.** Report everything. Attend the annual refresher. Follow emergency procedures. Watch out for one another.

## Common employee questions — and how to answer them

**"Do I have to sign the acknowledgment?"**
Yes. Sign-off is a training record required by Cal/OSHA. It confirms you received the plan and completed training — not that you agree with anything bad happening to you.

**"What counts as 'violence' for reporting?"**
Any act or threat of violence at work. That includes verbal threats, physical intimidation, objects thrown, credible statements about harm — even if no one was physically hurt.

**"What if I report something and I am wrong?"**
The law protects good-faith reports. You will not be punished for reporting something that turns out to be a misunderstanding.

**"What if my supervisor is the problem?"**
Use the anonymous hotline, go to HR, or go to the safety coordinator. Cal/OSHA is also a valid path (1-833-579-0927).

**"Will my name end up in the log?"**
No. The Violent Incident Log keeps personal details out by law. It records what happened, when, and where — never who.

**"Do customer complaints count?"**
Regular complaints, no. Threats, verbal abuse, refusal-to-leave incidents, or situations where a staff member feels unsafe — yes, report those.

**"What about telework?"**
The plan covers you anywhere you perform work, including your home office and field sites.

**"How often do I have to train?"**
Once at hire. Once a year after that. Any time the plan changes materially.

## Escalation triggers — call the safety coordinator immediately

Some situations should not wait for an end-of-shift report. Escalate at once if any of the following happen:

- **Weapon present or implied.** Any visible weapon or statement about having one.
- **Specific threat.** A named target, named time, or named method of harm.
- **Physical contact.** Pushing, hitting, throwing objects, restraining.
- **Stalking patterns.** Repeated unwanted contact from a current or former partner showing up at work.
- **Suicidal statements** by anyone on premises.
- **Trespass with refusal to leave** after being asked by a supervisor.
- **Pattern of escalation** over a few days — grievance rising into hostility.

For any of these: call the safety coordinator. If the threat is immediate, call 911 first.

## After-action checklist for managers

Within 24 hours of any incident or threat you handle:

- Secure the scene and make sure medical help was offered.
- Notify the safety coordinator so the Violent Incident Log entry can be opened.
- Document the **what, when, where** — not the **who** (names go in a separate, confidential file).
- Review the immediate controls — panic buttons, lighting, door locks, camera coverage.
- Offer a debrief to affected staff. Refer to the employee assistance program (EAP) if available.
- Follow up with the reporter within 5 business days. Tell them what was done.

## Monthly manager rhythm

- **Monday huddles:** 60-second reminder of one reporting channel or one procedure.
- **Mid-month:** One-on-one with each direct report — ask "any safety concerns lately?"
- **End of month:** Review the list of open corrective actions with the safety coordinator.

## What you should not do

- **Do not promise anonymity you cannot deliver.** If state law requires disclosure in a specific case, say so up front.
- **Do not retaliate — or let anyone on your team retaliate** — against a reporter. This is the fastest way to turn a small incident into a Cal/OSHA citation.
- **Do not delay reports** waiting for "more information." Log first, investigate second.
- **Do not handle a weapon or active-threat situation alone.** Call 911 and evacuate.

## Who to contact

- **Safety coordinator:** named in the plan (check the cover page)
- **HR lead:** named in the plan
- **Anonymous hotline:** listed in the plan
- **Cal/OSHA:** 1-833-579-0927 or dir.ca.gov/dosh
- **911:** for any immediate danger
