## 1. OSHA At-A-Glance (OSHA 3439-B-12R 2014)

**Category:** General Overview
**Industry:** All (General Industry, Construction, Maritime, Agriculture)

### Employer Obligations
- Follow all relevant OSHA safety and health standards
- Find and correct safety and health hazards
- Inform employees about chemical hazards (training, labels, alarms, color-coded systems, SDS)
- **Incident Reporting:** Notify OSHA within **8 hours** of a workplace fatality; within **24 hours** of any work-related inpatient hospitalization, amputation, or loss of an eye (1-800-321-OSHA / osha.gov/report_online)
- Provide required PPE at **no cost** to workers
- Keep accurate records of work-related injuries and illnesses
- Post OSHA citations, injury/illness summary data, and "Job Safety and Health — It's The Law" poster
- **No retaliation** against workers exercising rights

### Employee Rights
- Safe workplace conditions
- Training in a language they understand
- Review injury/illness records
- Get copies of hazard test results
- File confidential OSHA complaints
- **Anti-retaliation:** File complaint within **30 days** of alleged retaliation

### OSHA Standards Categories
- Construction, General Industry, Maritime, Agriculture
- Limit hazardous chemical exposure
- Require safe practices, equipment, monitoring
- **General Duty Clause:** Workplaces must be free of serious recognized hazards (cited when no specific standard applies)

### Inspection Priorities (ranked)
1. Imminent danger
2. Catastrophes (fatalities/hospitalizations)
3. Worker complaints and referrals
4. Targeted inspections (particular hazards, high injury rates)
5. Follow-up inspections

### Violation & Citation Process
- Inspector finds violations → OSHA may issue citations and fines
- Citations include corrective methods + deadline
- Employers can contest any part
- Workers can challenge deadlines only
- Appeals → Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission

### Coverage
- **Private sector:** All 50 states + DC + territories
- **State/local government:** Only in OSHA-approved state plan states
- **Federal agencies:** OSHA monitors but does not fine
- **Not covered:** Self-employed; hazards regulated by other federal agencies (MSHA, DOE, Coast Guard)

### OSHA-Approved State Plans
AK, AZ, CA, CT, HI, IA, IL, IN, KY, MD, MI, MN, NC, NJ, NM, NV, NY, OR, SC, TN, UT, VA, VT, WA, WY, Puerto Rico, Virgin Islands
(CT, IL, NJ, NY, VI cover public sector only)

### Employer Assistance Programs
- **Free On-Site Consultation:** Small businesses (<250 workers/site, <500 nationwide). Separate from enforcement, no penalties.
- **Compliance Assistance Specialists:** General info on standards
- **Alliance Program:** Works with groups for compliance resources
- **OSHA Strategic Partnerships (OSP):** Tailored agreements
- **Voluntary Protection Programs (VPP):** Recognizes employers with effective programs, injury rates below national average

## 2. Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (BBFACT01 / DSG 1/2011)

**Category:** Health Hazard — Biological
**Standard:** 29 CFR 1910.1030
**Industry:** General Industry (healthcare, labs, any occupational exposure to blood/OPIM)

### Pathogens Covered
- Hepatitis B Virus (HBV)
- Hepatitis C Virus (HCV)
- Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)

### Employer Requirements
1. **Exposure Control Plan** (written) — eliminate/minimize occupational exposures
   - List job classifications with occupational exposure
   - List tasks/procedures causing exposure
   - **Update annually** — reflect changes + document consideration of safer devices + solicit frontline worker input
2. **Universal Precautions** — treat all human blood/OPIM as infectious
3. **Engineering Controls** — sharps disposal containers, self-sheathing needles, safer medical devices (engineered sharps-injury protection, needleless systems)
4. **Work Practice Controls** — handling/disposing contaminated sharps, specimens, laundry, cleaning surfaces
5. **PPE** — gloves, gowns, eye protection, masks (employer cleans/repairs/replaces at no cost)
6. **Hepatitis B Vaccination** — offered to all workers with occupational exposure within **10 days** of initial assignment
7. **Post-Exposure Evaluation & Follow-Up** — at no cost (document route of exposure, test source individual, test worker's blood, offer prophylaxis, counseling)
8. **Labels & Signs** — warning labels on regulated waste containers, contaminated sharps, refrigerators with blood/OPIM, contaminated equipment/laundry. Red bags/containers as alternative.
9. **Training** — initial assignment + at least annually + when new tasks affect exposure. Must be in language workers understand.
10. **Recordkeeping** — medical records, training records, sharps injury log

## 7. Lockout/Tagout Fact Sheet (OSHAFS3529 / DEP FS-3529 12/2022)

**Category:** Hazardous Energy Control
**Standard:** 29 CFR 1910.147 (Control of Hazardous Energy)
**Additional:** 29 CFR 1910.333 (Electrical safe work practices)
**Industry:** General Industry

### Hazardous Energy Types Controlled
- Electrical, Mechanical, Hydraulic, Pneumatic, Chemical, Thermal, and other energy sources

### Statistics
- Compliance prevents **120 fatalities** and **50,000 injuries** annually
- Average **24 workdays** lost per injury from hazardous energy exposure
- ~3 million workers who service equipment face greatest risk (craft workers, machine operators, laborers)

### Employer Requirements
1. Develop, implement, and enforce an **energy control program**
2. Use **lockout devices** for equipment that can be locked out (tagout only if lockout is not possible AND provides equivalent protection)
3. Ensure new/overhauled equipment is **capable of being locked out**
4. Develop, document, implement, and enforce **energy control procedures** (exception: see note to 29 CFR 1910.147(c)(4)(i))
5. Use only **authorized lockout/tagout devices** — durable, standardized, substantial
6. Devices must **identify individual users**
7. Only the worker who applied a device may remove it (exception: 29 CFR 1910.147(e)(3))
8. **Inspect** energy control procedures at least **annually**
9. Provide **effective training** for all covered workers
10. Additional provisions: testing/repositioning, outside contractors, group lockout, shift/personnel changes

### Training Must Cover
- Employer's energy control program aspects
- Elements of energy control procedure relevant to employee
- Various OSHA lockout/tagout standard requirements

## 8. OSHA Inspections Fact Sheet (FACTSHEET-INSPECTIONS / DEP FS-3783 08/2016)

**Category:** Enforcement Process
**Industry:** All

### Inspection Priorities (6 levels)
1. **Imminent danger** — could cause death/serious physical harm (top priority)
2. **Severe injuries/illnesses** — fatalities (8hr), hospitalizations/amputations/eye loss (24hr)
3. **Worker complaints** — allegations of hazards (confidential)
4. **Referrals** — from other agencies, individuals, organizations, media
5. **Targeted inspections** — high-hazard industries, high injury rate workplaces
6. **Follow-up inspections** — abatement verification

### Phone/Fax Investigations
- Lower-priority hazards: OSHA may phone employer + fax details
- Employer must respond in writing within **5 working days**
- If response adequate and complainant satisfied → generally no on-site inspection

### On-Site Inspection Process
1. **Preparation** — research history, review operations, gather PPE/testing instruments
2. **Credentials** — officer presents photo ID + serial number
3. **Opening Conference** — explains why selected, scope, procedures, employee representation
4. **Walkaround** — inspect for hazards, review injury/illness records, check OSHA poster
5. **Closing Conference** — discuss findings, possible actions, consultation services, employee rights

### Violation Categories
- Willful
- Serious
- Other-than-serious
- De minimis
- Failure to abate
- Repeated

### Penalties & Appeals
- Citations must be issued within **6 months** of violation occurrence
- OSHA reduces penalties for: small employers, good faith (except willful violations)
- Employers get **15 working days** to formally contest
- Contest → forwarded to Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission
- Unchallenged citations become final order of OSHRC

## 9. Fall Prevention Fact Sheet (OSHA 3533-04 / NIOSH 2012-142)

**Category:** Fall Protection — Quick Reference
**Industry:** Construction

### Falls from Roofs Checklist
- Wear harness, always stay connected
- Ensure harness fits
- Use guardrails or lifelines
- Inspect all fall protection equipment before use
- Guard or cover all holes, openings, and skylights

### Don'ts
- Don't disconnect from lifeline
- Don't use defective equipment
- Don't work around unprotected openings or skylights

### Falls from Ladders Checklist
- Choose right ladder for the job
- Maintain 3 points of contact
- Secure the ladder
- Always face the ladder

### Ladder Don'ts
- Don't overreach
- Don't stand on top/top step of stepladder
- Don't place on unlevel footing

### Falls from Scaffolds Checklist
- Use fully planked scaffolds
- Ensure proper access
- Plumb and level
- Complete ALL guardrails
- Ensure stable footing
- Inspect before use (by competent person)

### Scaffold Don'ts
- Don't stand on guardrails
- Don't climb cross-braces
- Don't use ladder on top of scaffold

### Universal Principle
**PLAN** ahead to get job done safely. **PROVIDE** right equipment. **TRAIN** everyone to use it safely.

## 10. Fall Protection in Construction (OSHA 3146-05R 2015)

**Category:** Fall Protection — Comprehensive Guide (48 pages)
**Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart M (1926.500, 1926.501, 1926.502, 1926.503)
**Industry:** Construction

### Key Trigger
- Falls are **#1 cause of fatalities** in construction (~1/3 of all fatalities)
- 2013: 291 fatal falls to lower level out of 828 total construction fatalities

### The 6-Foot Rule
Fall protection required when workers are at **6 feet or more** above a lower level.
Exception: Less than 6 feet when near dangerous equipment (open drive belts, pulleys, gears, degreasing vats, acid)

### Covered Areas & Activities
- Ramps, runways, walkways
- Excavation edges
- Hoist areas
- Steep roofs
- Wall openings
- Holes (including skylights)
- Unprotected sides/edges
- Above dangerous equipment
- Leading edges under construction
- Formwork and reinforcing steel

### Conventional Fall Protection Systems

#### Guardrail Systems (29 CFR 1926.502(b))
- Top rails: **42 inches ±3 inches** above walking surface
- Midrails: midway between top rail and walking surface
- Must withstand **200 lbs** force at top edge
- Midrails must withstand **150 lbs**
- No openings >19 inches wide
- Steel/plastic banding prohibited for rails
- Min 1/4 inch diameter/thickness
- Wire rope: flagged every 6 feet with high-visibility material

#### Safety Net Systems (29 CFR 1926.502(c))
- Install as close as practicable under work surface, never >30 feet below
- Minimum horizontal distance table:
  - Up to 5 ft vertical: 8 ft horizontal
  - 5-10 ft vertical: 10 ft horizontal
  - >10 ft vertical: 13 ft horizontal
- Mesh openings: max 36 sq in, max 6 in per side
- Border rope: min **5,000 lbs** breaking strength
- Inspect weekly; remove items before next shift

#### Personal Fall Arrest Systems (29 CFR 1926.502(d))
- Components: **A**nchorage, **B**ody harness, **C**onnectors (ABC)
- Body belts **prohibited** for fall arrest (since Jan 1, 1998)
- Max arresting force: **1,800 lbs** with body harness
- Max free fall: **6 feet**
- Max deceleration distance: **3.5 feet**
- System strength: 2x potential impact energy
- Snaphooks: must be **locking type**
- Vertical lifelines/lanyards: min **5,000 lbs** breaking strength
- Self-retracting (≤2 ft free fall): min **3,000 lbs** tensile
- Self-retracting (>2 ft free fall): min **5,000 lbs** tensile
- Anchorages: **5,000 lbs per worker** or 2x expected impact load
- Ropes/straps: **synthetic fibers** only
- Inspect before each use

### Positioning Device Systems (29 CFR 1926.502(e))
- Max free fall: **2 feet**
- Anchorage: **3,000 lbs** or 2x potential impact load

### Fall Restraint Systems
- Prevents fall of any distance (tethers worker)
- Min capacity: **3,000 lbs** or 2x max expected force

### Additional Systems

#### Warning Line Systems (29 CFR 1926.502(f))
- Erected around all sides of roof work areas
- Min 6 ft from edge (no mechanical equipment)
- Min 6 ft parallel / 10 ft perpendicular (with mechanical equipment)
- Flagged every 6 ft with high-visibility material
- Height: 34-39 inches
- Min tensile strength: **500 lbs**

#### Controlled Access Zones (29 CFR 1926.502(g))
- 6-25 ft from unprotected/leading edge (general)
- 6-60 ft or half member length (precast concrete)
- 10-15 ft from working edge (overhand bricklaying)
- Control line height: 39-45 in (50 in for overhand bricklaying)
- Min breaking strength: **200 lbs**

#### Safety Monitoring Systems (29 CFR 1926.502(h))
- Alternative for low-slope roofing only
- Competent person designated as monitor
- Must be on same surface, see workers, speak directly, no other duties

### Activity-Specific Requirements

| Activity | CFR | Requirements |
|----------|-----|-------------|
| Leading Edges | 1926.501(b)(2) | Guardrails, nets, or PFAS at 6+ ft |
| Overhand Bricklaying | 1926.501(b)(9) | Guardrails/nets/PFAS OR controlled access zone; >10" below surface requires PFAS |
| Low-Slope Roofs (≤4:12) | 1926.501(b)(10) | Guardrails, nets, PFAS, warning line + conventional, or warning line + safety monitor |
| Steep Roofs (>4:12) | 1926.501(b)(11) | Guardrails with toeboards, nets, or PFAS |
| Residential Construction | 1926.501(b)(13) | Conventional fall protection; fall protection plan if infeasible |
| Hoist Areas | 1926.501(b)(3) | Guardrails or PFAS at 6+ ft |
| Holes/Skylights | 1926.501(b)(4) | PFAS, covers, or guardrails (>6 ft); covers for tripping hazard |
| Ramps/Runways | 1926.501(b)(6) | Guardrails at 6+ ft |
| Excavations | 1926.501(b)(7) | Guardrails, fences, barricades, or covers at 6+ ft |
| Dangerous Equipment | 1926.501(b)(8) | Guardrails/nets/PFAS at 6+ ft; guardrails or guards at <6 ft |
| Wall Openings | 1926.501(b)(14) | Guardrails, nets, or PFAS when bottom edge 6+ ft above lower level and <39" above walking surface |

### Falling Object Protection
- Guardrails: openings small enough to prevent passage
- Overhand bricklaying: no materials within 4 ft of edges
- Roofing: no materials within 6 ft of edge (unless guardrails)
- Toeboards: min 3.5 inches tall, withstand 50 lbs, max 0.25" clearance

### Fall Protection Plans (29 CFR 1926.502(k))
- Only for: leading edge, precast concrete, residential construction
- Only when conventional protection is infeasible or creates greater hazard
- Prepared by qualified person, site-specific
- Must document reasons, alternative measures, identify locations
- Competent person supervises implementation
- Update after falls or serious incidents

### Training (29 CFR 1926.503)
- All workers exposed to fall hazards
- Competent person delivers training on: hazard nature, erecting/maintaining/inspecting systems, operation of all protection types, safety monitoring roles, mechanical equipment limitations, materials handling, fall protection plan roles, Subpart M requirements
- Written certification record required (name, dates, trainer signature)
- Retrain when: workplace changes, equipment changes, inadequate knowledge demonstrated

### Key Definitions
- **Competent person:** Can identify existing/predictable hazards, authorized to take corrective measures
- **Qualified person:** Recognized degree/certificate/standing, or extensive knowledge/training/experience
- **Hole:** Gap/void ≥2 inches in least dimension
- **Opening:** Gap/void ≥30 inches high, ≥18 inches wide in wall/partition
- **Low-slope roof:** ≤4:12
- **Steep roof:** >4:12

## 11. Forklift Safety Quick Card (OSHA 3949-04R 2019)

**Category:** Powered Industrial Trucks
**Industry:** General Industry, Warehousing

### Operations Safety
- Operate per manufacturer's instructions
- Always wear seatbelt (when equipped)
- Never exceed rated load; ensure stable/balanced
- Don't raise/lower load while traveling
- Safe distance from platform/ramp edges
- Be aware of other vehicles
- Clear visibility + enough clearance
- Proper footing + handhold when entering
- Use horns at cross aisles and obstructed areas
- Watch for pedestrians, observe speed limit
- No rides on forks; don't lift people with forks

### Training
- Only **trained and certified** workers may operate
- Operators trained on specific truck types in use

### Maintenance
- Remove unsafe forklifts from service
- Keep clean (free of excess oil/grease)
- Repair per manufacturer recommendations

## 12. Workplace Mental Health (OSHA 4395-01 2024)

**Category:** Mental Health & Wellness
**Industry:** All

### Key Topics

#### Traumatic Events
- Explosions, chemical releases, building/equipment collapses, co-worker injuries/deaths, abuse/assault
- Symptoms: anxiety, sadness, anger, flashbacks, nightmares, confusion, sleep problems, easily frightened
- Can lead to acute stress disorder or PTSD (require professional help)

#### Substance Use Disorder
- Persistent desire for substances despite negative consequences
- Can be treated and controlled
- Related to chronic pain, injuries, other mental health conditions

#### Suicide Statistics (CDC 2021)
- 12M+ adults seriously think about suicide/year
- 3M+ adults plan suicide/year
- 1M+ adults attempt suicide/year
- 48,000+ people die by suicide/year in US

#### Risk Factors
- Mental health conditions (depression, bipolar, schizophrenia, anxiety, substance use)
- Traumatic events, chronic pain/illness, prolonged stress
- Recent tragedy/loss, criminal/legal problems
- Job loss/financial problems, childhood trauma, domestic violence

#### Employer Actions
- Prioritize mental health as part of workplace culture
- Have conversations about it
- Know how to support workers needing assistance

#### Resources
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** — call or text 988 (24/7)
- Veterans Crisis Line: text 838255
- Crisis Text Line: text Talk to 741741 (English), Ayuda to 741741 (Spanish)

### Awareness Months
- May: Mental Health Awareness Month
- June: PTSD Awareness Month
- September: National Suicide Prevention Month + National Recovery Month

## 13. Electric-Arc Flash Hazards (OSHA 4472-11 2024)

**Category:** Electrical Safety
**Industry:** General Industry, Construction, Maritime, Power Generation/Transmission/Distribution

### Key Standards Referenced
- 29 CFR 1910.269 (Electric power generation, transmission, distribution)
- 29 CFR 1910.301–399 (Subpart S — Electrical, General Industry)
- 29 CFR 1910.332 (Electrical training)
- 29 CFR 1910.333(b)(1) (De-energized but not locked out = energized)
- 29 CFR 1910.334 (Use of electrical equipment)
- 29 CFR 1910.335 (Safeguards — electrical)
- 29 CFR 1910.399 (Definitions — qualified person)
- 29 CFR 1910.132 (PPE general requirements)
- 29 CFR 1910.147 (Lockout/tagout)
- 29 CFR 1926.400–449 (Subpart K — Electrical, Construction)
- 29 CFR 1926.950–968 (Subpart V — Power Transmission/Distribution)
- 29 CFR 1926.95 & 1926.97 (PPE — Construction)
- 29 CFR 1926.960(c)(1) and (g)(2) and (g)(5) (Approach distances, incident energy, PPE)
- NFPA 70E (Standard for Electrical Safety in the Workplace)
- IEEE 1584 (Arc flash calculation methods)

### Arc Flash Key Facts
- Temperatures exceed **35,000°F** (19,400°C) — 4x surface of sun
- **5-10 arc flash explosions** occur daily in US (NFPA 70E estimate)
- **3,600+ disabling** electrical contact injuries/year
- Most burns from **ignited flammable clothing**, not arc itself
- Items within **3 feet** burn/melt/vaporize

### Critical Myths Debunked
1. "I don't need AR PPE because I'm not doing energized work" — **Wrong.** De-energized ≠ electrically safe working condition (ESWC). De-energizing is just one step.
2. "My justified energized work is safe" — **Wrong.** Strict safety measures still required.

### Three Boundary Types

| Boundary | Purpose | Key Rules |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Restricted Approach** | Electric shock (highest risk) | Unqualified workers NEVER cross. Qualified workers: PPE + insulated objects only. |
| **Limited Approach** | Electric shock hazard exists | Unqualified workers supervised by qualified + accompanied. |
| **Arc Flash Boundary** | 2nd-degree burn risk from arc | May be inside OR outside limited/restricted boundaries. Unqualified workers: appropriate PPE + close supervision. |

**Critical:** Arc flash boundary does NOT follow a fixed relationship to shock boundaries. It can be LARGER than limited approach boundary.

### Arc Flash PPE Categories (NFPA 70E 2021)

| Category | Min Arc Rating | Key AR Clothing | Key Protective Equipment |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| 1 | 4 cal/cm² | AR long-sleeve shirt/pants or overalls, AR face shield or hood | Hard hat, safety glasses, hearing protection, leather gloves or rubber insulating gloves |
| 2 | 8 cal/cm² | AR shirt/pants or overalls, AR hood or face shield + balaclava | Hard hat, safety glasses, hearing protection, rubber insulating gloves with leather protectors |
| 3 | 25 cal/cm² | AR shirt + pants + overall + flash suit jacket/pants/hood, AR gloves | Hard hat, safety glasses, hearing protection, leather footwear |
| 4 | 40 cal/cm² | AR shirt + pants + overall + flash suit jacket/pants/hood, AR gloves | Hard hat, safety glasses, hearing protection, leather footwear |
| 5 (CSA Z462) | 75 cal/cm² | (Canadian standard extension) | — |

### Hierarchy of Controls for Arc Flash
1. Elimination (most effective)
2. Substitution
3. Engineering Controls
4. Administrative Controls (training, permits, barricading, procedures)
5. PPE (least effective — last resort)

### Administrative Controls
- Step-by-step procedures for workers exposed to arc flash
- Barricade arc flash boundary with caution/danger tape
- Comprehensive electrical safety training
- Qualified/certified workers for electrical tasks
- Permit-to-work system for energized work

### Electrical Standards Organization

|----------|-------------|------------------|

**Category:** Master Training Requirements Compilation (270 pages)
**Industry:** ALL (General Industry, Maritime, Construction, Agriculture, Federal)

- Initial assignment

### 16. Recommended Practices for Safety & Health Programs (OSHA 3885 — Oct 2016)

**Category:** Safety & Health Program Framework (Recommended Practices — not enforceable)
**Industry:** General Industry (NOT construction — separate publication exists)
**Pages:** 40

#### Standards Referenced
- 29 CFR 1904 (Injury/illness recordkeeping)
- 29 CFR 1904.35(b)(1)(iv) (Anti-retaliation in reporting)
- 29 CFR 1904.39 (Special reporting: fatality 8hrs, amputation/eye loss/hospitalization 24hrs)
- Section 11(c) of OSH Act of 1970 (Anti-retaliation)

#### 7 Core Elements
1. **Management Leadership** — written policy, measurable goals, resource allocation, accountability
2. **Worker Participation** — encourage participation, reporting (anonymous option), access to info (SDSs, injury data), involve in all aspects, remove barriers
3. **Hazard Identification & Assessment** — collect existing info, inspect workplace, identify health hazards (chemical/physical/biological/ergonomic), investigate incidents (including near misses), characterize/prioritize
4. **Hazard Prevention & Control** — hierarchy of controls (elimination > substitution > engineering > administrative > PPE), develop hazard control plan, follow up on effectiveness
5. **Education & Training** — program awareness for all, supervisor-specific, worker role-specific, hazard-specific, retrain when conditions change
6. **Program Evaluation & Improvement** — track leading indicators (participation, suggestions, response times, training completion) + lagging indicators (injuries, comp data), verify annually, correct shortcomings
7. **Communication & Coordination** — multi-employer sites (host employers, contractors, staffing agencies, temp workers)

#### 10-Step Quick-Start Checklist
1. Set safety/health as top priority
2. Lead by example
3. Implement reporting system (include anonymous option)
4. Provide training
5. Conduct inspections with workers
6. Collect hazard control ideas from workers
7. Implement controls (let workers choose/implement/evaluate)
8. Address emergencies (scenarios, instructions, drills)
9. Seek input on workplace changes
10. Make improvements (regular safety discussions)

#### Key Data
- Ohio SHARP: claims -52%, cost/claim -80%, lost time -87%, claims per $M payroll -88%
- Indirect costs of incidents: min **2.7x direct costs**

#### Incentive Program Warning
- Incentive programs must NOT discourage injury/illness reporting
- Mandatory post-injury drug testing can suppress reporting (OSHA memos Mar 2012, Oct 2018)

#### Glossary (key terms for Protekon knowledge base)
- Competent person, qualified person, serious hazards, close call/near miss, JHA, hierarchy of controls, leading/lagging indicators, PtD (Prevention through Design), SHARP, VPP, root cause analysis, SDS
